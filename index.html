A Study on the Radioactivity of Curium

With 96 protons, 96 electrons, and an average of 247 neutrons, curium proves to be one of the more interesting elements on the Periodic Table.

Origin, Synthesis, and Decay

An artificially produced element, curium was initially discovered and syntehsized in 1944 at the University of California, Berkeley. It has been produced in particle accelerators by ramming lighter elements with alpha particles, the most common being Pu-239 to produce Cm-242 and a neutron.One of the most common isotopes of curium, Cm-242 has a half-life of 162.8 days and undergoes alpha decay to produce Pu-238.

Uses

  • Since Cm-242 and Cm-247 can produce as much as 120 W/g and 3 W/g, respectively, they're commonly used in radioisotope thermoelectric generators, like those in spacecraft.
  • With its long half-life, Cm-242 can be used to produce Pu-238, which is used in cardiac peacemakers.
  • Cm-244 is commonly used in alpha particle X-ray spectrometers, used in space and Mars exploration missions.

Hazards

Even though curium usually emits alpha particles during their decay, some if its decay products emit significant amounts of beta and gamma radiation. If consumed, the portion of curium that is not excreted travels to the blood, liver, and the bones. This stops bone marrow production and red blood cell creation, leading to bone tumors and liver cancer When inhaled, curium has also been known to cause lung cancer.

Did You Know?

  • Curium is so radioactive that it glows in the dark.
  • Cm-247, the most "stable" of the radioisotopes, has a half-life of 15,600,000 years.
  • Because of its unique structure, C-242 can undergo spontaneous fission.
  • Cm-242 generates about three watts of thermal energy per gram, more than plutonium produces (yes, the element used in A-bombs :) ).
